1. Archaeological Methods and Reconnaissance

This section introduces how archaeologists locate, investigate, and map sites without excavation. Key methods include desktop studies (historical documents, maps, SMR/NMR records), surface surveys (fieldwalking, building recording, micro-contour analysis), geophysical techniques (resistivity, magnetometry), and aerial photography (cropmarks, soilmarks, shadow sites). The book explains when each method is appropriate and how archaeologists design sampling strategies to avoid bias.

2. Excavation and Post-Excavation Analysis

Students learn why archaeologists excavate, the difference between research, rescue, and salvage excavations, and how to record contexts, plans, sections, and photographs. Post-excavation chapters outline laboratory techniques for analysing artefacts and ecofacts, including ceramics, lithics, metals, soils, faunal remains, human remains, and plant macro/microfossils. It highlights how scientific tools such as XRF, NAA, petrology, radiocarbon dating, and dendrochronology contribute to reconstructing the past.

3. Interpretation, Transformation Processes, and Managing the Past

The book explains the challenges of interpreting archaeological data: formation processes, post-depositional factors, and recovery biases all shape what survives. It explores how archaeologists use ethnography, actualistic studies, and historical accounts to make sense of evidence. It also addresses threats to archaeological heritage—development, agriculture, climate—and describes modern frameworks for site protection, including planning regulations, legislation, rescue archaeology, and the role of museums, societies, and professional units.

4. Major Themes in Archaeology: Religion, Ritual, Settlement, Society, and Material Culture

  • Religion and ritual: identifying ritual sites, funerary practices, rites of passage, sacred landscapes, religious specialists.
  • Settlement archaeology: understanding landscapes, reconstructing environments, spatial organisation, functional interpretation of sites.
  • Material culture and economics: subsistence strategies, domestication, storage, craft production, trade and exchange systems.
  • Social archaeology: political organisation, power, status, rank, gender, population, ethnicity, and social change.

Each theme includes case studies (e.g., Karnak, Copan, West Kennet, Tell Abu Hureyra) that illustrate analytical approaches.

5. Presenting Archaeological Knowledge

  • Archaeology in nationalism, identity, and land rights
  • Ethical debates (e.g., Native American repatriation, Seahenge)
  • Modes of presentation: museums, reconstructions, media, digital technologies
  • Communication skills, including evaluating displays through the “4 As” (audience, accuracy, access, aesthetics)

Students learn to critique different forms of archaeological storytelling.

6. Study Skills, Projects, and Exam Preparation

  • How to choose, research, and write an archaeological project

  • Understanding command words and mark schemes
